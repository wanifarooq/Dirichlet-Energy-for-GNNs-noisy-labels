\begin{abstract}
Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) are vulnerable to label noise in graph classification, yet the mechanisms driving this vulnerability are poorly understood. We empirically identify a property of the \emph{learned representations} that tracks noise memorization: the within-graph Dirichlet energy of node features. Energy decreases as the model captures genuine class structure, then rises sharply, mainly in high-frequency components, when the model begins to memorize corrupted graph labels. We support this observation with a Poincar\'e-based lower bound: under a lower-Lipschitz regularity assumption on the encoder, fitting noisy graph-level labels for two structurally similar graphs forces a lower bound on their within-graph Dirichlet energies. Guided by this, suppressing the within-graph energy growth should block the noise-fitting path. We design and evaluate three interventions: a Dirichlet-energy regularizer ($\mathcal{L}_{DE}$), a robust loss (\method{}), and a spectral weight constraint (CE+W2). Across eight benchmarks, including the full and modified ogbg-ppa, symmetric noise up to $60\%$, asymmetric noise, and a transfer to Cora node classification, the three interventions all reduce within-graph energy and improve robustness while preserving clean-data performance. \method{}, our practical recommendation, matches or exceeds OMG~\citep{omg7} at lower training overhead.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) are now the workhorse of \emph{graph classification}, the task of assigning a single label to an entire graph, with applications spanning molecular property prediction~\citep{NCI}, bioinformatics~\citep{proteins}, and social-network analysis~\citep{COLLAB}. In real deployments, the labels attached to graphs are routinely noisy: assays disagree, annotators err, and weak supervision injects systematic mistakes. Although learning under label noise has been thoroughly studied for images~\citep{algan2021image} and for node classification~\citep{nrgnn11,contrastivelearning5}, graph classification under noisy labels is dramatically less explored~\citep{graphC1, omg7}.

\textbf{Graph classification is a fundamentally different setting.} In node classification, label noise corrupts \emph{individual node labels} on a single graph, and homophily, the tendency of connected nodes to share labels, is a structural prior that directly couples Dirichlet energy of the \emph{label signal} to noise. In graph classification, label noise flips the label of an \emph{entire graph}: nodes have no individual classification labels, and within-graph homophily/heterophily is largely orthogonal to the graph-level label. The standard tools and intuitions from node classification therefore do not transfer.

\textbf{Our central observation.} We measure the Dirichlet energy of \emph{learned node representations}, not of labels. Empirically, this energy first decreases as the model captures genuine, transferable patterns; then, when the model begins to memorize incorrect graph-level labels, it rises sharply, with the surge concentrated in the high-frequency spectrum. This rise is not a tautology: noise is injected only at the graph-label level, while node features and edges are unchanged, so any correlation between energy and labels has to emerge through training dynamics. Theorem~\ref{thm:noise_forces_energy} supports this observation analytically: under a lower-Lipschitz regularity assumption on the encoder, fitting noisy graph-level labels for two structurally similar graphs forces a lower bound on their within-graph Dirichlet energies via the Graph Poincar\'e inequality.

\textbf{Why does suppressing this energy growth help?} Theorem~\ref{thm:noise_forces_energy} shows that fitting graph-level label noise forces within-graph Dirichlet energy to increase. Suppressing this growth therefore plausibly blocks the noise-fitting path, a hypothesis we test with three mechanistically distinct interventions and validate empirically across eight benchmarks.

\textbf{Contributions.}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=0.5pt,topsep=2pt]
    \item A controlled study of \emph{when} GNNs fit graph-level label noise (Section~\ref{sec:failure_modes}), isolating three failure modes: over-parameterization, low-order graphs, and small training sets.
    \item Within-graph Dirichlet energy as a diagnostic for graph-level noise memorization (Section~\ref{sec:diagnostic}), with a frequency decomposition that localizes the surge to high-frequency components and an empirical scaling with the noise rate.
    \item Analytical support for the diagnostic (Section~\ref{sec:theory}): a spectral identity (Proposition~\ref{prop:dataset_energy}) and a Poincar\'e-based lower bound under noise fitting (Theorem~\ref{thm:noise_forces_energy}). The bound is unique to graph classification, where labels and feature smoothness live on different objects, unlike node classification.
    \item Three interventions (Section~\ref{sec:methods}): a Dirichlet-energy regularizer $\mathcal{L}_{DE}$, a centroid-based loss \method{}, and a spectral weight constraint CE+W2. They are mechanistically distinct and all reduce within-graph energy while improving robustness; \method{} is our practical recommendation.
    \item Evaluation on eight benchmarks under symmetric, asymmetric, and high-rate ($60\%$) noise, the full and a modified ogbg-ppa, and a transfer to Cora node classification. \method{} matches or exceeds OMG~\citep{omg7} and SOP~\citep{liu2022robusttraininglabelnoise} at $\sim$70$\times$ lower compute.
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\textbf{Learning under label noise.} Robust loss functions~\citep{ghosh2017robust,patrini2017making}, sample re-weighting~\citep{liu2015classification,liu2022robusttraininglabelnoise}, early-learning regularizers~\citep{arpit2017closer}, and small-loss filtering~\citep{gui2021towards} dominate the image-classification literature. \citet{wani2023combining} introduce NCOD, a centroid-based outlier-discounting loss that we adapt to graphs.

\textbf{Noisy-label learning on graphs.} Most prior work concerns \emph{node} classification, exploiting homophily, contrastive losses, or pseudo-labels~\citep{nrgnn11,contrastivelearning5,ssl8,cl9,Kang2018RobustGL}. For \emph{graph} classification, \citet{graphC1} propose a surrogate loss that discards estimated-noisy labels under specific assumptions, and OMG~\citep{omg7} combines contrastive learning, MixUp~\citep{mixup2}, and curriculum filtering. We differ in two ways: (i) we provide a spectral/energy-based explanation of \emph{why} GNNs fit graph-level noise, rather than only mitigating it, and (ii) our practical method (\method{}) achieves better or comparable robustness at a fraction of the compute.

\textbf{Dirichlet energy and over-smoothing.} Dirichlet energy quantifies signal smoothness on graphs~\citep{dirichlet}. Existing analyses establish that GNNs act as low-pass filters whose energy decays exponentially with depth under spectral conditions~\citep{nt2019revisiting,noteonoversmoothing,oversmoothing2,rusch2023survey}, and that weight-matrix eigenvalues control attraction/repulsion of node features~\citep{GRAFF}. Energy-based regularizers~\citep{zhou2021dirichlet,FAGCN,EEConv} address \emph{too little} energy (over-smoothing) on \emph{node} classification. We address the opposite regime: \emph{too much} within-graph energy caused by graph-level label noise. \citet{exploreOversmoothing} recently study over-smoothing for graph classification through a structural-entropy lens; an explicit quantitative connection between structural entropy and noise-driven sharpening is left as an open direction. A more comprehensive related-work discussion is in Appendix~\ref{app:additional_related}.

\section{Background}
\label{sec:background}

\textbf{Notation.} A graph is $\mathcal{G}=(\mathcal{V},\mathcal{E},\mathbf{X})$ with $N=|\mathcal{V}|$, degree matrix $\mathbf{D}$, adjacency $\mathbf{A}\in\{0,1\}^{N\times N}$, feature matrix $\mathbf{X}\in\mathbb{R}^{N\times m}$, combinatorial Laplacian $\mathbf{L}=\mathbf{D}-\mathbf{A}$ (with eigenvalues $0=\lambda_1\le\lambda_2\le\dots\le\lambda_N$) and normalized Laplacian $\mathbf{\Delta}=\mathbf{D}^{-1/2}\mathbf{L}\mathbf{D}^{-1/2}$. We write $\lambda_2(\mathcal{G})$ for $\lambda_2(\mathbf{L})$ throughout and use $\mathbf{\Delta}$ only when explicitly stated. In \emph{graph classification}, the dataset is $\mathcal{D}=\{(\mathcal{G}^i,\mathbf{y}_i)\}_{i=1}^n$, $\mathbf{y}_i\in\{0,1\}^{|C|}$ the one-hot graph label; we write $c_i$ for the class. Under message passing, $\mathbf{H}_i^{l+1}=\mathrm{UP}_{\mathbf{\Omega}_l}(\mathbf{H}_i^l,\mathrm{AGGR}_{\mathbf{W}_l}(\mathbf{H}_i^l,\mathbf{A}^i))$, with $\mathbf{H}_i^0\equiv\mathbf{X}^i$ and $\mathbf{Z}^i\equiv\mathbf{H}_i^L$. A permutation-invariant readout $f_\theta:\mathbb{R}^{N^i\times m'}\to\mathbb{R}^{|C|}$ maps node features to class probabilities $\hat{\mathbf{y}}_i$. Under noisy labels, the observed $c_i$ may differ from the ground truth; the test set is clean.

\textbf{Dirichlet energy on graphs.} For graph $\mathcal{G}^i$ with representation $\mathbf{Z}^i\in\mathbb{R}^{N^i\times m'}$ we use the combinatorial Dirichlet energy
\begin{equation}
\Edir(\mathbf{Z}^i)=\sum_{(u,v)\in\mathcal{E}^i}\bigl\|\mathbf{Z}^i_u-\mathbf{Z}^i_v\bigr\|_2^2 = \mathrm{tr}\bigl((\mathbf{Z}^i)^\top\mathbf{L}^i\mathbf{Z}^i\bigr).
\label{eq:edir}
\end{equation}
Small $\Edir$ means smooth (low-frequency) representations within the graph; large $\Edir$ means sharpening (high-frequency). The symmetrically-normalized variant $\Edir_{\mathrm{sym}}(\mathbf{Z})=\sum_{(u,v)}\|\mathbf{Z}_u/\sqrt{d_u}-\mathbf{Z}_v/\sqrt{d_v}\|_2^2 = \mathrm{tr}(\mathbf{Z}^\top\mathbf{\Delta}\mathbf{Z})$, used in some over-smoothing analyses~\citep{nt2019revisiting,oversmoothing2}, is equivalent up to degree-bounded constants and our qualitative claims hold for either; we use \eqref{eq:edir} because it pairs cleanly with the Poincar\'e inequality of Section~\ref{sec:theory}. To track behavior across a class-restricted subset $\mathcal{S}$ of training graphs we use
\begin{equation}
\Edirset(\mathcal{S})=\frac{1}{|\mathcal{S}|}\sum_{\mathcal{G}^i\in\mathcal{S}}\Edir(\mathbf{Z}^i).
\label{eq:edirset}
\end{equation}
Crucially, \eqref{eq:edir} is computed on \emph{learned representations}, not on labels. Within-graph homophily of features therefore evolves with training, decoupled from the graph-level label of $\mathcal{G}^i$.

\section{When GNNs Fit Graph-Label Noise}
\label{sec:failure_modes}

GNNs trained with standard cross-entropy (CE) on graph classification tasks are not uniformly vulnerable to label noise. On the full ogbg-ppa~\citep{PPA} (37 classes, the standard OGB benchmark), even $40\%$ symmetric noise barely degrades CE accuracy, because the model is under-parameterized for the full task and behaves robustly~\citep{memorizenoise}. To expose the noise-fitting regime, we control three axes: \textbf{(F1)} number of classes (proxy for over-parameterization), \textbf{(F2)} graph order (size of each graph), and \textbf{(F3)} training-set size. We use a 5-layer GIN~\citep{WL1} with $300$ hidden units throughout, unless stated.

We track $\Edirset(\mathcal{D}_c)$, the within-graph energy averaged over training graphs of class $c$. Across architectures and datasets (Fig.~\ref{fig:diagnostic}), the diagnostic is consistent: $\Edirset$ falls during the early ``learning'' phase, then rises precisely when the model begins to fit noisy graphs (CE-20\% in Fig.~\ref{fig:diag_b}), with $40\%$ noise yielding a strictly larger end-of-training energy than $10\%$ noise (Appendix~\ref{app:additional_studies}).

\textbf{Energy is sensitive to noise specifically through high-frequency components.} A natural concern is that energy may also rises during clean-data overfitting~\citep{2sides}; we confirm this in Appendix~\ref{app:additional_studies} but show the noise-driven surge is markedly steeper. To localize the surge spectrally, we use the HLFF-GNN decomposition~\citep{hlffgnn} to split representations into low-frequency ($Z_1$) and high-frequency ($Z_2$) components. On ENZYMES with $30\%$ noise, $\Edir(Z_1)$ remains essentially unchanged, while $\Edir(Z_2)$ rises monotonically, and slope/variance statistics of $\Edir(Z_2)$ provide an early warning for label noise (Appendix~\ref{app:amplification}). HLFF-GNN is used purely as an analytical tool; the same total-energy surge appears in standard GIN/GCN.

\textbf{Why energy is more useful than a generic overfitting signal.} Standard noise-detection signals require a \emph{clean} held-out set: validation loss is uninformative when validation labels follow the same noise distribution as training (the noisy validation loss continues to decrease as the model fits noise, as we observe in our setting); train--test gap is unobservable at training time; small-loss heuristics need a calibration regime. Within-graph Dirichlet energy is computed entirely on the training graphs and their representations, so it remains a valid noise-fitting indicator under fully-noisy supervision. Beyond replacing the missing clean signal, it tells you \emph{how} the network is failing: which spectral components drive the surge. This mechanistic specificity is what turns a diagnostic into a design principle, and motivates the three interventions in Section~\ref{sec:methods}, each of which targets the same harmful spectral behavior in a distinct way.

\section{Analytical Support: Why Energy Suppression Helps}
\label{sec:theory}

Section~\ref{sec:diagnostic} established Dirichlet energy as a reliable empirical signal. We now show this is not just correlation: under standard regularity, within-graph energy is \emph{provably forced} to grow when a GNN fits graph-level label noise. The argument combines three pieces: a dataset-level spectral identity (Proposition~\ref{prop:dataset_energy}), the Graph Poincar\'e inequality (Lemma~\ref{lem:poincare}), and a lower-Lipschitz bound on the GNN (Lemma~\ref{lem:bilip})~\citep{chuang2022tree, davidson2024h, juvina2024training}, yielding Theorem~\ref{thm:noise_forces_energy}.

\subsection{Three Building Blocks}

\begin{proposition}[Dataset-level Dirichlet energy]
\label{prop:dataset_energy}
Let $\mathcal{D}=\{\mathcal{G}^1,\dots,\mathcal{G}^n\}$ with representations $\mathbf{Z}^i\in\mathbb{R}^{N^i\times m'}$ and adjacencies $\mathbf{A}^i$. Define the block-diagonal graph $\bar{\mathcal{G}}=(\bar{\mathbf{Z}},\bar{\mathbf{A}})$ with $\bar{\mathbf{Z}}=[\mathbf{Z}^1\|\dots\|\mathbf{Z}^n]\in\mathbb{R}^{N_{\text{tot}}\times m'}$ (vertical concatenation) and $\bar{\mathbf{A}}=\mathrm{blkdiag}(\mathbf{A}^1,\dots,\mathbf{A}^n)$, with associated combinatorial Laplacian $\bar{\mathbf{L}}=\mathrm{blkdiag}(\mathbf{L}^1,\dots,\mathbf{L}^n)$. Then
\begin{equation}
\Edirset(\mathcal{D})=\tfrac{1}{|\mathcal{D}|}\Edir(\bar{\mathbf{Z}})=\tfrac{1}{|\mathcal{D}|}\!\!\sum_{r=1}^{m'}\sum_{u=1}^{N_{\text{tot}}}\bar{\lambda}_u(\bar{\bm{\psi}}_u^\top\bar{\mathbf{Z}}_{:,r})^2,
\label{eq:spectral_dataset}
\end{equation}
where $\{(\bar{\lambda}_u,\bar{\bm{\psi}}_u)\}$ are eigenpairs of $\bar{\mathbf{L}}$, and the spectrum decomposes as $\bar{\Lambda}=\bigcup_i\Lambda^i$ with each eigenvector supported on a single connected component $\mathcal{G}^i$ (graph or block).
\end{proposition}

The proof (Appendix~\ref{app:proofs}) follows from the block-diagonal structure of $\bar{\mathbf{L}}$. The key structural fact: \emph{each high-frequency eigenvector of $\bar{\mathbf{L}}$ is owned by exactly one graph}, so sharpening along it raises only that graph's energy.

\begin{lemma}[Graph Poincar\'e, vector-valued form]
\label{lem:poincare}
For any feature matrix $\mathbf{Z}\in\mathbb{R}^{N\times m'}$ on a connected graph $\mathcal{G}$ with combinatorial Laplacian $\mathbf{L}=\mathbf{D}-\mathbf{A}$ and second-smallest eigenvalue $\lambda_2(\mathcal{G})>0$, let $\bar{\mathbf{z}}=\frac{1}{N}\sum_v\mathbf{Z}_v$. Then
\begin{equation}
\sum_{v=1}^N\|\mathbf{Z}_v-\bar{\mathbf{z}}\|_2^2\;\le\;\frac{1}{\lambda_2(\mathcal{G})}\,\Edir(\mathbf{Z}).
\label{eq:poincare}
\end{equation}
\end{lemma}

\eqref{eq:poincare} bounds within-graph feature variance by Dirichlet energy scaled by the spectral gap; proof by Courant--Fischer per feature dimension (Appendix~\ref{app:proofs}).

\begin{lemma}[Lower Lipschitz bound for a GIN block with 2-layer combine, adapted from~\citealp{chuang2022tree, davidson2024h, juvina2024training}]
\label{lem:bilip}
Let $h_\theta(\mathbf{X})=\sigma_2\bigl(\sigma_1(\mathbf{M}\mathbf{X}\mathbf{W}_1)\mathbf{W}_2\bigr)$ be a single GIN aggregation $\mathbf{M}=(1+\varepsilon)\mathbf{I}+\mathbf{A}\in\mathbb{R}^{N\times N}$ followed by a two-layer MLP with weights $\mathbf{W}_1\in\mathbb{R}^{m\times m'}$ and $\mathbf{W}_2\in\mathbb{R}^{m'\times m''}$ acting on the feature dimension, with the widening regime $m\leq m'\leq m''$ and full row rank weights. Assume $\sigma_{\min}(\mathbf{M})\geq\kappa>0$ (e.g., $1+\varepsilon>|\lambda_{\min}(\mathbf{A})|$), $\sigma_{\min}(\mathbf{W}_k)\geq m_k>0$, and that $\sigma_1,\sigma_2$ are leaky-ReLU with slope $\alpha>0$. Then
\begin{equation}
\|h_\theta(\mathbf{X}+\Delta\mathbf{X})-h_\theta(\mathbf{X})\|_F\;\geq\;c_{\text{lower}}\,\|\Delta\mathbf{X}\|_F,\qquad c_{\text{lower}}=\alpha^2 m_1 m_2 \kappa.
\label{eq:bilip}
\end{equation}
For ordinary ReLU the chain applies locally with $\alpha=1$ on segments where all preactivations are strictly positive, giving $c_{\text{lower}}=m_1 m_2 \kappa$. A true two-layer GIN $\sigma_2(\mathbf{M}\sigma_1(\mathbf{M}\mathbf{X}\mathbf{W}_1)\mathbf{W}_2)$ replaces $c_{\text{lower}}$ by $\alpha^2 m_1 m_2 \kappa^2$.
\end{lemma}

Proof: chain the global element-wise lower-Lipschitz bound of leaky-ReLU ($\|\sigma_k(\mathbf{Y}+\Delta)-\sigma_k(\mathbf{Y})\|_F\geq\alpha\|\Delta\|_F$) with $\sigma_{\min}$ of $\mathbf{W}_2,\mathbf{W}_1,\mathbf{M}$ via $\sigma_{\min}(\mathbf{P}\otimes\mathbf{Q})=\sigma_{\min}(\mathbf{P})\sigma_{\min}(\mathbf{Q})$ (Appendix~\ref{app:proofs}). The lemma encodes that the GIN does not collapse meaningful feature differences under injective aggregation and feature transforms, a property the model can exploit when fitting noisy labels.

\subsection{Main Theorem: Noise Fitting Forces Energy Growth}

Consider two graphs $\mathcal{G}^i,\mathcal{G}^j$ with $N^i=N^j=N$ and similar pooled descriptors $\mathbf{g}_i\approx\mathbf{g}_j$ on the clean distribution (same-$N$ for notational simplicity; unequal $N$ in Appendix~\ref{app:proofs}). Suppose label noise flips $c_j$ to $c_i'\neq c_i$, forcing different graph-level outputs. Recall $\mathbf{Z}^i\in\mathbb{R}^{N\times m'}$ from Section~\ref{sec:background}; let $\mathbf{g}_i=\frac{1}{N}\sum_v\mathbf{Z}^i_v$ and $\widetilde{\mathbf{Z}}^i:=\mathbf{1}_N\mathbf{g}_i^\top$ (constant-signal replication of $\mathbf{g}_i$).

\begin{theorem}[Graph-level noise fitting forces $\Edir$ to grow]
\label{thm:noise_forces_energy}
Under the hypotheses of Lemmas~\ref{lem:poincare}--\ref{lem:bilip} and $N^i=N^j=N$,
\begin{equation}
\|\mathbf{Z}^i-\mathbf{Z}^j\|_F \;\le\; \sqrt{N}\,\|\mathbf{g}_i-\mathbf{g}_j\|_2 + \sqrt{\tfrac{\Edir(\mathbf{Z}^i)}{\lambda_2(\mathcal{G}^i)}}+\sqrt{\tfrac{\Edir(\mathbf{Z}^j)}{\lambda_2(\mathcal{G}^j)}}.
\label{eq:noise_bound}
\end{equation}
In particular, if the noisy training objective combined with an $L$-Lipschitz readout forces a graph-level output gap of at least $\Delta_{\text{out}}$, so that $\|\mathbf{Z}^i-\mathbf{Z}^j\|_F\geq\Theta_{\text{noise}}:=L^{-1}\Delta_{\text{out}}$, while the structural similarity gives $\|\mathbf{g}_i-\mathbf{g}_j\|_2\leq\varepsilon$, then
\begin{equation}
\sqrt{\tfrac{\Edir(\mathbf{Z}^i)}{\lambda_2(\mathcal{G}^i)}}+\sqrt{\tfrac{\Edir(\mathbf{Z}^j)}{\lambda_2(\mathcal{G}^j)}} \;\geq\; \Theta_{\text{noise}}-\sqrt{N}\,\varepsilon.
\label{eq:energy_lower}
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}

\textbf{Reading the bound.} \eqref{eq:energy_lower} lower-bounds the within-graph Dirichlet energies. The intuition is geometric: for two structurally similar graphs mapped to different labels, $\mathbf{g}_i\approx\mathbf{g}_j$, so the required output gap $\Theta_{\text{noise}}$ cannot come from the means alone; by Poincar\'e, the only remaining route is the within-graph variance $\Edir/\lambda_2$. The unequal-$N$ case (zero-padded matching) is handled in Appendix~\ref{app:proofs}. The bound has no node-classification analog: there labels live on the same nodes whose smoothness $\Edir$ measures, so any link is trivial by construction.

\textbf{Empirical anchor.} The bound predicts that $\sqrt{\Edir/\lambda_2}$ should grow monotonically with the noise rate, since higher noise forces the encoder to discriminate more pairs of similar graphs. The PPA noise-rate sweep in Appendix~\ref{app:additional_studies} confirms this: asymptotic $\Edirset$ at $10\%,20\%,30\%,40\%$ noise is monotonically increasing.

\textbf{Implication.} Theorem~\ref{thm:noise_forces_energy} establishes that fitting graph-level label noise forces within-graph Dirichlet energy to increase. Suppressing this growth therefore blocks the noise-fitting path; we validate this empirically in Section~\ref{sec:experiments}. An explicit quantitative connection between which graphs absorb most of the energy growth and structural properties such as Shannon entropy~\citep{exploreOversmoothing} remains an open question.

\section{Three Methods that Reduce Within-Graph Energy}
\label{sec:methods}

The diagnostic and analytical results above suggest controlling within-graph Dirichlet energy through any of three mechanistically distinct levers: a regularizer on the energy itself, a loss that down-weights samples driving the energy up, or an architectural projection that removes the spectral directions responsible for sharpening. We instantiate one intervention per lever. \method{} is our practical recommendation; CE+W2 is included as a theoretical instantiation.

\subsection{Explicit Dirichlet-Energy Regularization ($\mathcal{L}_{DE}$)}

The most direct lever is to penalize $\Edir(\mathbf{Z}^i)$ above a class-aware threshold $U_{c_i}$:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{DE}(\mathcal{D})=\frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^N\bigl[\max(0,\Edir(\mathbf{Z}^i)-U_{c_i})\bigr]^2,\quad
\mathcal{L}=\mathcal{L}_{CE}+\lambda\,\mathcal{L}_{DE}.
\label{eq:lde}
\end{equation}
We instantiate the threshold in two ways. \textbf{Class-specific:} $U_c$ is recomputed every epoch as the mean $\Edir$ over a small clean validation subset of class $c$. This adapts to class-dependent baseline energies (a molecular graph's natural energy differs from a social graph's), but requires clean held-out data. \textbf{Fixed:} $U_c=U$ globally, a single hyperparameter, no clean validation needed, at the cost of mild over-smoothing on clean labels. Both variants improve robustness; class-specific additionally improves \emph{clean-label} accuracy by acting as a generalization-aware regularizer.

\subsection{Graph Centroid Outlier Discounting (\method{})}

\method{} is our practical recommendation. It extends NCOD~\citep{wani2023combining} (originally an image-classification loss) to graph classification, with two design changes that are necessary in the graph-classification regime: a third KL term that disentangles clean from noisy graphs via per-sample trainable parameters $\mathbf{u}\in[0,1]^n$, and feedback through the current training accuracy $a_{\text{train}}$ that prevents premature outlier discounting. For batch $B$ with predictions $f_\theta(\mathbf{Z}_B)$, hard predictions $\hat{\mathbf{y}}_B$, soft labels $\tilde{\mathbf{y}}_B$ (centroid-based, see Appendix~\ref{app:gcod}), and observed labels $\mathbf{y}_B$:
\begin{align}
\mathcal{L}_1 &= \mathcal{L}_{CE}\bigl(f_\theta(\mathbf{Z}_B)+a_{\text{train}}\,\mathrm{diag}_{\text{vec}}(\mathbf{u}_B)\,\mathbf{y}_B,\;\tilde{\mathbf{y}}_B\bigr),\label{eq:gcod1}\\
\mathcal{L}_2 &= \frac{1}{|C|}\bigl\|\hat{\mathbf{y}}_B+\mathrm{diag}_{\text{vec}}(\mathbf{u}_B)\,\mathbf{y}_B-\mathbf{y}_B\bigr\|^2,\label{eq:gcod2}\\
\mathcal{L}_3 &= (1-a_{\text{train}})\,D_{KL}\bigl\{\mathfrak{L},\;\sigma(-\log\mathbf{u}_B)\bigr\},\label{eq:gcod3}
\end{align}
with $\mathfrak{L}=\log\sigma(\mathrm{diag}_{\text{mat}}(f_\theta(\mathbf{Z}_B)\mathbf{y}_B^\top))$. Updates are split: $\theta\leftarrow\theta-\alpha\nabla_\theta(\mathcal{L}_1+\mathcal{L}_3)$, $\mathbf{u}\leftarrow\mathbf{u}-\beta\nabla_{\mathbf{u}}\mathcal{L}_2$. \method{} is not a theoretically derived energy minimizer. Empirically, however, it reduces $\Edirset$ during training (Fig.~\ref{fig:diagnostic}) without explicitly penalizing it: by down-weighting graphs flagged as noisy via large $u_i$, it removes the gradient pressure that would otherwise push the network toward high-frequency directions, exactly as Theorem~\ref{thm:noise_forces_energy} predicts for any sample-level mechanism that suppresses noise-driven gradients.

\subsection{Spectral Constraint on Weight Matrices (CE+W2)}

A third lever, included as a theoretical instantiation, acts directly on the weights. \citet{GRAFF} show that for linear graph convolutions with symmetric $\mathbf{W}$, the gradient flow of $\Edir$ decomposes along eigenvectors of $\mathbf{W}$: positive eigenvalues smooth, negative ones sharpen. Practical $\mathbf{W}_l^2$ is not symmetric, so we split $\mathbf{W}_l^2=\mathbf{S}_l+\mathbf{N}_l$ and project only $\mathbf{S}_l$ to its positive eigenspace, leaving the skew $\mathbf{N}_l$ (purely imaginary spectrum, rotational) untouched. This is a heuristic extension of GRAFF — the energy of $\mathbf{M}\mathbf{Z}\mathbf{W}$ depends on $\mathbf{W}^\top\mathbf{W}$, not just $\mathbf{S}_l$ — but the resulting CE+W2 needs neither clean validation nor extra hyperparameters and shows the energy mechanism can be controlled architecturally. Implementation details are in Appendix~\ref{app:weights_eigens}; the per-epoch eigendecomposition is costly and can destabilize convergence, so we recommend $\mathcal{L}_{DE}$ or \method{} for practical use.

\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}

\textbf{Setup.} We evaluate on ogbg-ppa~\citep{PPA} (full and a controlled $30\%$/$6$-class subset), MUTAG, PROTEINS, ENZYMES, IMDB-BINARY, REDDIT-MULTI-12K, MSRC-21, MNIST-graph, and Cora (node classification, transfer test).\footnote{Anonymized code: \url{https://anonymous.4open.science/r/Robustness_Graph_Classification-E76F/}.} Backbones are GIN~\citep{WL1} and GCN~\citep{gcn}; full hyperparameters are in Appendix~\ref{app:experimental_setting}. We compare against three baselines: standard cross-entropy (CE); SOP~\citep{liu2022robusttraininglabelnoise}, a strong noise-robust loss from image classification; and OMG~\citep{omg7}, the leading graph-classification noise method. All numbers are mean$\pm$std over $4$ runs.

\textbf{Why a controlled PPA subset?} On the full $37$-class PPA, all methods are under-parameterized for the task, and CE itself is robust (Section~\ref{sec:failure_modes}); a noise-robustness method cannot help where the baseline does not fail. We use a $30\%$/$6$-class PPA subset as a \emph{controlled} testbed where over-parameterization triggers noise fitting, and we additionally report the full-PPA result to show our methods do no harm there. Generality claims are grounded in the seven additional benchmarks.

\section{Conclusion}

Within-graph Dirichlet energy diagnoses graph-level noise memorization, supported by a Poincar\'e-based lower bound (Theorem~\ref{thm:noise_forces_energy}) unique to graph classification. Three interventions ($\mathcal{L}_{DE}$, \method{}, CE+W2) reduce energy and improve robustness on eight benchmarks; \method{} matches OMG at $\sim$70$\times$ lower compute. \textbf{Limitations:} sufficient bound only, synthetic noise, heuristic CE+W2. \textbf{Impact:} robust GNNs aid chemistry/biology; \method{} needs no clean validation.
